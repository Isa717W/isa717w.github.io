The Ocean of Accents: Moana’s Balancing Act Between Myth and Realitys

Disney’s Moana (2016) attempts to celebrate Polynesian culture but stumbles in its linguistic representation. Maui, the demigod voiced by Dwayne Johnson, speaks with a Polynesian-accented English marked by glottal stops (e.g., “Maui” [ˈmɑ.ʔui]) and elongated vowels. These features nod to Indigenous Pacific languages, yet they homogenize diverse Polynesian dialects into a pan-regional “mythic” accent. Meanwhile, the villain Tamatoa’s campy New Zealand English (/ɪ/ → [ə] in “fish” [fəʃ]) ties his flamboyance to queer-coding, reducing LGBTQ+ identity to vocal theatrics.

Lippi-Green’s critique of Disney’s “foreign accent = exotic” trope looms large here. Maui’s accent, while culturally inspired, lacks specificity—erasing distinctions between Hawaiian, Māori, and Samoan speech patterns. This flattening, as Brous (2021) argues, reduces Polynesia to a monolithic “fantasy” realm, divorced from modern Indigenous realities. Even Moana’s near-GAE, with slight Polynesian inflection, prioritizes Western relatability over authentic representation.

Tamatoa’s accent introduces another layer of stereotyping. His New Zealand English, performed by Jemaine Clement, leans into exaggerated diphthongs and a theatrical delivery that aligns with Western perceptions of queerness as inherently humorous or villainous. The /ɪ/ → [ə] shift (“fish” [fəʃ]) and languid prosody frame him as a decadent, narcissistic figure, perpetuating the trope that non-normative speech signifies moral decay.

The film’s most glaring omission is its treatment of the Oceanic languages. While Moana incorporates Tokelauan lyrics in “We Know the Way,” the spoken dialogue remains overwhelmingly English-dominant. This erasure of Indigenous languages in favor of accented English reflects what scholar Soares (2017) calls “cultural cherry-picking”—using superficial linguistic elements to signal diversity without engaging deeply with marginalized voices.

Moana’s heartwarming narrative of self-discovery is undermined by its linguistic shortcuts. By blending Polynesian accents into a vague “mythic” past and queering Tamatoa’s villainy through speech, Disney prioritizes marketable exoticism over authenticity. Until the studio commits to nuanced, community-guided representation, even well-intentioned films like Moana will drift in shallow waters.
