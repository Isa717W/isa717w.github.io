Prey or Predator? How Zootopia’s Accents Reinforce Real-World Biases

Zootopia (2016), hailed for its allegory on prejudice, ironically replicates the very biases it critiques through accent coding. Protagonist Judy Hopps’ General American English (GAE)—rhotic /r/, neutral vowels—positions her as an aspirational hero, while antagonist Gideon Gray’s Southern drawl (“right” [raːt]) ties rural foxes to ignorance. The film’s “melting pot” setting masks a rigid linguistic hierarchy where marginalized accents signify moral or intellectual deficiency.

Valleriani’s (2020) analysis of Zootopia highlights how dubbing practices globalize stereotypes. In the Italian version, Duke Weaselton’s AAVE is replaced with a Neapolitan accent, linking Southern Italian dialects to criminality. Similarly, Mr. Big’s Spanish-influenced English (“’em” for “them”) frames him as a mafia boss, exoticizing foreignness as inherently corrupt. Gazelle’s South American-accented English, with rolled /r/ in “beautiful” ([bea̯ˈt̪iful]), reduces her to a hypersexualized performer, echoing Lippi-Green’s (2012) critique of Latina-coded “spicy” tropes.

Even secondary characters reinforce biases. The polar bear drill instructor’s Southern accent (“dead” [dɛːd]) and Clawhauser’s “gay voice” (high pitch, elongated vowels) reduce regional and LGBTQ+ identities to punchlines. Judy’s parents, despite their rural roots, speak near-GAE—erasing their potential Southern markers to avoid tainting her image. This selective accent use, as Anderson (2019) argues, reveals Disney’s discomfort with non-mainstream speech in heroic roles.

Zootopia’s attempt to address systemic bias is undermined by its own linguistic shortcuts. By equating accents with archetypes—Southerners as bullies, foreigners as criminals, queerness as comic—the film mirrors real-world discrimination. Until Disney confronts how accent hierarchies perpetuate oppression, even its most “woke” narratives will remain toothless.
