A Scottish Rebellion: Authenticity or Caricature in Brave?

Pixar's Brave (2012) ventures into Scotland's rugged landscapes with protagonist Merida, whose Scottish English accent symbolizes her defiance of traditional gender roles. Voiced by Kelly Macdonald, Merida's speech features rhotic /r/ sounds (e.g., “bear” [bɛɹ]) and distinct vowel shifts, such as /ʌ/ → [ʊ] in “blood” [blʊd]. These markers aim to root her character in Scottish linguistic identity, contrasting with her mother Queen Elinor's Received Pronunciation (RP), which reflects regal formality. While Merida's accent celebrates regional authenticity, the film's portrayal of Scottishness teeters between reverence and caricature.

The Witch, a comic relief character, embodies this tension. Her exaggerated Scots dialect includes rolled /r/ sounds and the iconic /x/ in “loch” ([lɔx]), phonetic traits rarely heard in modern urban Scotland. This hyper-stylized speech, paired with her erratic behavior, reduces her to a mystical oddity rather than a nuanced figure. Linguists like Lippi-Green (2012) argue that such exaggerated accents in Disney films often serve to “other” characters, framing them as comic or primitive. The Witch's dialect, while humorous, reinforces the trope that non-standard accents are inherently unserious or uncouth.

Brave's linguistic choices reflect a romanticized view of Scotland. Merida's accent, though broadly Scottish, lacks specificity—erasing distinctions between Highland, Lowland, and urban dialects. The film's setting, inspired by medieval Scotland, sidesteps contemporary linguistic diversity, instead presenting a homogenized “timeless” Celtic identity. Scholar Roberts (2022) notes that such flattening exoticizes cultures, reducing them to aesthetic props rather than living traditions.

Queen Elinor's RP accent further underscores class hierarchies. Her refined speech contrasts with the broader Scots of secondary characters, subtly associating RP with authority and sophistication. This mirrors real-world biases where “prestige” accents are valorized over regional ones. Even Merida's rebellion is framed through her accent: her Scottish English becomes a metaphor for resistance, yet the film avoids critiquing the systemic marginalization of regional dialects.

While Brave is praised for centering a strong female lead, its linguistic representation remains uneven. Merida's accent empowers her character but exists within a narrative that exoticizes Scottish culture. The Witch's dialect, though entertaining, perpetuates the idea that marked accents are best suited for comic roles. For a film about breaking traditions, Brave clings to outdated linguistic stereotypes, proving that even progressive stories can falter in their portrayal of diversity.
